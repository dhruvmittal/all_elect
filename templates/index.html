Once again, you find yourself smack-dab in the middle of a US election cycle (with a presidential election cycle on the order of 600 days, it's almost as likely as not), and all sorts of people are saying all sorts of things about what "the people" think.

These are veteran pollsters, you think as you refresh RealClear Politics for the seventh time this morning. I should be able to trust them.

But why, then, do none of them seem to agree with each other? How do you know who to believe?

"Polling is an art," they say, but that doesn't seem quite right to you. It's 2016 and you see no reason not to put your modern number-crunching know-how to work analysing pollsters.

You read a quote from a guy who sounds like he knows what he's talking about.

You're inspired! This looks like a problem you can tackle!

"We think there should be more pollster ratings."
Nate Silver

First, you need to know if pollsters are actually consistent. Can we actually differentiate between pollsters based on some aggregated feature?

Accuracy, you suspect, would be a good indicator of a pollster's overall performance. After all, if one follows all the "best practices" of the art, one really should get the best results.

You rush over to RCP and scrape all their polls on Primaries. You know that the difficulty in polling primaries increases non-linearly the further before the contest date you poll, which suggests that a time-series might contain the interesting information.

Nice! Plotting the aggregate accuracy over all accessible primaries of only the fifteen most prolific pollsters, you see some differentiation. There's every indication that your game might be the right one to play.

But even a bad pollster can get lucky. And a bad pollster might have every incentive to cheat! Thus, accuracy cannot be the end of the story.

As you brainstorm interesting features, you try to address questions you would actually have about each pollster. You wonder if you can determine whether a pollster has a partisan bias.

Of course, a lot of polls are taken by manifestly partisan organizations, but that shouldn't be your yardstick. After all, even the parties themselves need some unbiased measure of their candidate's performance.

You pull data on the last three presidential elections and test pollsters results against the mean results for the respective contests.

Plotting only the most prolific pollsters, you again see reasonable differentiation (and the reassurance that the bias chart lines up with your intuition).

But really, you need more data. You find a database of polls on complete contests available for download from FiveThirtyEight. A few quick analyses suggest to you that the same sorts of statistics are manifest within this set as well, lending credence to the idea that they well describe the true statistics of the problem.

You try to cluster pollsters into groups with similar performace based on these sorts of features. KMeans is your friend here, but you don't like the built-in version very much. You implement it in TensorFlow.

Though you cluster in a higher-dimensional featurespace, you find yourself with the disadvantage of only being able to comprehend three spacial dimensions at a time. Projecting down onto a subset of your featurespace, you can visualize the clustering reasonably well.

You're left with a great big table of Pollsters organized into similar performance categories, using features such as:

  • Accuracy: a number between 0 and 1 indicating the proportion of contests for which their polls successfully identified the victor Higher is better.
  • Mean Sample Size: on average, how many people do they poll? Higher is better.
  • Mean Error: How far are they from calling the correct spread between the victor and the first runner up? Lower is better.
  • Mean Reverted Bias: Historical average statistical bias, calculated by reverting to the mean of each contest. Positive values correspond to Democratic bias; Negative, Republicans. Closer to zero is better.
  • Also reported is N, the number of published polls in the 538 dataset by this pollster. Note that the pollsters with fewer than 20 polls published in the past several years have been excluded from my calculation.
  • Finally, the grade as assigned by the clustering algorithm.
